# Brotherhood Formation Guide — Working Outline

**Status:** DRAFT · planning document, not yet a public asset
**Target format:** 30-day devotional + discipleship workbook
**Audience:** Christian men stepping into leadership — at home, at work, in church, in their community
**Distribution (target):** PDF (free or low-cost) → print-on-demand workbook → expanded into a 90-day cohort over time
**Price target:** $14.99 digital (as currently listed on /index.html); free chapter sampler

---

## Why this book, and why now

The site has been promising a Brotherhood Formation Guide for months under a "Coming Soon" badge. That badge is becoming a credibility tax — the longer it sits, the more it signals that the ministry talks more than it ships. The fix isn't to retire the badge. The fix is to ship the book.

The need is real: men in their 20s through 50s are starving for a discipleship resource that isn't either (a) recycled men's-conference fluff, or (b) academic enough to require a seminary degree. There's a middle lane — biblically literate, militarily disciplined, brotherhood-shaped, plainly written — and very few books occupy it. U.S.M.C. Ministries has the voice and the audience to plant a flag there.

The 30-day format is chosen deliberately. A month is long enough to form a habit and reshape a man's interior life, but short enough that a reader will actually finish. The pacing also maps onto the four pillars of U.S.M.C. (Uniting, Serving, Mentoring, Counseling) with one week per pillar plus a brief sending section — so the book teaches the ministry's framework by living inside it.

## Structure

### Front matter
- Foreword (1-2 pages) — from Adam, voice: direct first-person
- How to use this book (1 page) — solo daily reading, group cadence, or weekend retreat compression
- A note on translation — explain why the book quotes the version it does (KJV by default per the site's existing pronoun-reverence rule, with optional ESV/NKJV cross-references)
- Scripture index (back matter, generated last)

### Body — 30 daily readings

Each day is structured identically so the reader knows what to expect:

1. **Day heading** — Day N · Week X · Theme
2. **Scripture** — primary passage (4-12 verses), KJV with capitalized reverential pronouns per the site's house style
3. **Devotional** — 700-900 words. Voice: first-person plural ("we"), direct, biblically reasoned, MOOP language where it fits naturally
4. **Personal action item** — one concrete behavior to attempt before tomorrow
5. **Group question** — one open-ended question for a brotherhood discussion (if reading with others)
6. **Optional deep dive** — 1-2 sentence pointer to a longer resource on usmcmin.org (a lexicon entry, a blog post, a Bible plan)

### The 30-day arc

Each week is one letter of U.S.M.C., plus a 2-day sending close.

#### Week 1 — Days 1-7 · UNITING — "We Were Not Made to Stand Alone"

The first week dismantles the myth that mature manhood is solitary. Most men in the West have been catechized into a kind of stoic individualism that owes more to Marlboro ads than to Scripture. The Bible's portrait is the opposite: men are formed in covenant, with God first and with other men second.

The second week pivots from formation in community to formation through service. The leadership the world peddles is platform, profile, and power. The leadership Christ models is towel-and-basin. Every command we receive about leadership is downstream of how we serve the people no one's watching us serve.

The third week is about the chain — older to younger, faithful to faithful, generation to generation. Paul tells Timothy to invest in *men who will teach others also* (2 Tim 2:2). That's a 4-generation horizon in one verse. Most of us cannot name four men we are actively forming. That deficit is what this week confronts.

## Sample devotional — Day 1

This is the only fully-written day in the draft so far. It sets the voice. Every other day should match this tone, depth, and pacing.

### Day 1 · Week 1 · UNITING — The Man Alone Is Not Safe

**Scripture — Ecclesiastes 4:9-12 (KJV)**

> Two are better than one; because they have a good reward for their labour.
> For if they fall, the one will lift up his fellow: but woe to him that is alone when he falleth; for he hath not another to help him up.
> Again, if two lie together, then they have heat: but how can one be warm alone?
> And if one prevail against him, two shall withstand him; and a threefold cord is not quickly broken.

**Devotional**

There is a lie that has been sold to American men for the better part of a century, and it has cost us more than we know. The lie is that the strong man is the man who needs no one. The frontiersman. The cowboy. The lone wolf. The CEO who built it all himself, the war hero who fought through the night without a second, the father who provides without ever asking for help. The image is clean, and the marketing is good, but the image is not biblical. The man who stands alone is not the strong man. He is the man closest to falling.

Solomon — a king who could have had every advantage that wealth and crown could buy — looked at the world honestly and wrote down what he saw. *Two are better than one.* Not because two are sentimental, not because two are nice, but because two have a *good reward for their labour*. The math of the kingdom is not addition. It is multiplication.

Then he names the four hard places where being alone will break you.

First, the fall. *If they fall, the one will lift up his fellow.* Every man falls. The question is not whether — the question is who is there when it happens. The man alone falls and stays down. The man with a brother gets pulled back to his feet, sometimes whether he wants to be or not.

Second, the cold. *How can one be warm alone?* The cold here is not weather. It is the slow chill of a soul without warmth — the long evening of a marriage growing distant, the hollow ache of a faith going through the motions, the gray fog of work that has stopped meaning anything. A brother brings heat. Not because he fixes the cold, but because his presence makes the cold survivable.

Third, the attack. *If one prevail against him, two shall withstand him.* The enemy of your soul is patient. He picks his moments. He waits for the night you are isolated, exhausted, ashamed of something you have not told anyone. The man with brothers has men who will stand in the gap. The man alone fights the enemy alone, and he loses more often than he wins.

Fourth, the cord. *A threefold cord is not quickly broken.* Two strands can be untwisted. Three braided together hold. Notice the picture Solomon draws — not two men. Three. There is room in this picture for God Himself as the third strand, and there is also room for it to mean what it plainly means: a brotherhood of three is more durable than a partnership of two. Build your formation with that geometry in mind.

This week we will rebuild what most of us have allowed to crumble — the assumption that mature manhood is shared manhood. We will look at David and Jonathan, at Paul and Barnabas, at the table of the early church. We will look at the body of Christ as Paul describes it, where the eye cannot say to the hand *I have no need of thee*. By the end of the week the lie should be exposed for what it is, and the truth should have begun to put on flesh in your actual schedule, your actual phone, your actual table.

You were not made to stand alone. You will not stand alone. The only question is whether you will choose your brotherhood deliberately, or wait until you fall to discover who is there.

**Personal action item.** Before tomorrow, send a text to one man you trust. Tell him you are starting this guide. Ask him to read it with you — or, if he says no, ask him for permission to text him at the end of each week with what you are learning. You do not have to recruit a whole band today. You only have to break the silence.

**Group question.** Where in your life right now are you trying to stand alone, and what is it costing you?

**Deep dive (optional).** Read Hebrews 10:24-25 alongside today's passage and notice the active verbs — *provoke*, *consider*, *exhort*. Brotherhood is not passive proximity. It is mutual provocation toward love and good works.

## Voice & style guide

- **Person:** First-person plural ("we") for most teaching. First-person singular ("I") only when Adam speaks autobiographically. Second-person ("you") for direct charges to the reader.
- **Translation:** KJV primary. NKJV / ESV references in parentheses only when the modern reading clarifies meaning the KJV obscures.
- **Reverential pronouns:** Capitalize all personal pronouns referring to God, Christ, or the Spirit (He, Him, His, Me, My, You, Your when addressing God). Relative pronouns (who, whom) stay lowercase per the site's NKJV convention. This is already a site-wide style rule.
- **Length per day:** 700-900 words for the devotional itself. The whole day (heading + scripture + devotional + action + question + deep dive) lands around 1,200-1,400 words on the page. That's a 10-15 minute read at unhurried pace.
- **Sentences:** Smooth, connected prose. Avoid AI staccato. Use em-dashes and subordinate clauses to keep the rhythm. Long sentences are fine. Punchy 4-word lines are not.
- **Masculine register without machismo:** Direct, decisive, never preening. The book should feel like a respected older brother talking, not a drill instructor performing, and not a TED-talk pastor monetizing.
- **Concrete imagery:** Specifics beat abstractions. Towel and basin, not "humility." A text message to one man, not "intentionality." Stones of remembrance, not "reflection."
- **No filler.** If a sentence does not advance the argument, the example, or the worship, cut it.

## Production checklist (what's needed to ship a v0.1)

This is the path from this outline to a real downloadable PDF that retires the "Coming Soon" badge with integrity.

1. **Foreword + How to Use + Translation note** — about 1,500 words total. Adam writes these in his voice.
2. **29 more daily devotionals** matching Day 1's depth — roughly 23,000 words of body.
3. **Group leader's guide** — 8-12 pages on how to run a 30-day cohort or weekend retreat. About 3,000 words.
4. **Brotherhood covenant template** — one printable page.
5. **Scripture index + further reading list** — generated last.
6. **Layout** — typeset as a clean PDF (suggest 6x9 trim, Garamond or similar serif body, KJV blockquotes set apart visually). Tools: LaTeX, Pandoc + a TeX template, or Affinity Publisher.
7. **Cover** — the og-brotherhood.jpg already exists at `/assets/og/og-brotherhood.jpg`. A printable cover can be derived from it.
8. **Landing page** — `/brotherhood-formation-guide.html`, replacing the homepage card's "Coming soon" copy with a Buy Now / Download link once v0.1 exists.
9. **Sales page copy** — short, declarative, no fluff. Sample bullets:
   - 30 days · 4 weeks · 1 framework (Uniting · Serving · Mentoring · Counseling)
   - Built for solo reading, paired reading, or a weekend brotherhood retreat
   - Includes brotherhood covenant template
   - $14.99 digital / $24.99 paperback
10. **First cohort** — once v0.1 is out the door, soft-launch a 30-day Field Brief cohort: one daily email per day for 30 days. Costs nothing, surfaces feedback, builds the next edition.

A realistic v0.1 release path: 1 chapter per day for 4 weeks (29 days of writing) plus 3 days of front/back matter. If Adam writes one devotional per morning before the rest of the work starts, the book ships in five weeks.
