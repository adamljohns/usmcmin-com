Indiana Bailable Offenses and Substantial Risk Amendment (Nov 2026)

Statewide — All Indiana Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Adds a 'substantial risk' standard to the Indiana Constitution's bail clause: offenses other than murder or treason remain bailable unless the accused poses a substantial risk to any other person or to the community.
  • Lets a court deny pretrial release when the presumption of guilt is strong and the state proves no condition of release would protect the public.
  • Expands judicial authority to detain a defendant before trial beyond the current murder-and-treason exceptions.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Senate Joint Resolution 1. Indiana allows no citizen initiative; an amendment must pass two separately elected General Assemblies, then go to voters.
  • Second-passage votes (2026 session): Senate 43-2 and House 75-11; the resolution cleared the General Assembly February 17, 2026.
  • A simple statewide majority is required to ratify it on the November 3, 2026 ballot.

Who Supports This

  • Sen. Erich Koch (R-44), author
  • Rep. Chris Jeter (R-88)

Who Opposes This

  • Sen. Greg Taylor (D-33)
  • ACLU of Indiana
  • The Bail Project

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

Government bears the sword to be a terror to evil works and to protect the innocent, and there is a real, biblical case for keeping a genuinely dangerous defendant off the street while he awaits trial. That is the strength of this amendment. But the weight on the other side is just as serious: a man is presumed innocent until proven guilty, and 'substantial risk' is a phrase a judge must interpret — broaden it too far and you jail people who have been convicted of nothing. The safeguard written into the text is that the state must prove no release condition would work, which is meant to keep this narrow. Weigh it honestly: a 'yes' trades some pretrial liberty for community safety. Whether that trade is just depends on trusting Indiana's courts to apply 'substantial risk' soberly, not as a rubber stamp.
