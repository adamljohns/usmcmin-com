Kentucky Limit on Governor's Pardon Power Amendment (Nov 2026)

Statewide — All Kentucky Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Amends Section 77 of the Kentucky Constitution to suspend the governor's pardon and commutation power during a window around the gubernatorial election.
  • The window: 60 days before the gubernatorial election through the fifth Tuesday after it.
  • Treason clemency and reprieves are unaffected; the limit applies only to pardons and sentence commutations.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • SB 10 introduced: Jan. 8, 2026 by State Sen. Chris McDaniel (R-23).
  • Senate vote: 36-0 (Jan. 20, 2026). Unanimous.
  • House vote: 82-12 with amendments (April 1, 2026). 98.1% of voting Republicans yes, 54.2% of voting Democrats yes.
  • Senate concurrence on amendments: 36-1.
  • Kentucky requires a 60% supermajority of each chamber to refer a constitutional amendment; both chambers cleared that bar.

Background

The amendment follows national attention to end-of-term mass clemency actions in Kentucky, most prominently the 2019 last-day pardons issued by then-Gov. Matt Bevin, which drew bipartisan criticism. The bill sponsor explicitly framed the measure as a response to that pattern.

Who Supports This

  • State Sen. Chris McDaniel (R-23) — sponsor
  • Kentucky Senate Republicans (unanimous)

Who Opposes This

  • 12 Kentucky House Democrats voted no
  • Ballotpedia had not located an organized opposition campaign as of April 2026

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

This is a check-on-executive-power amendment, and an unusually bipartisan one. Christians who believe civil magistrates exist to administer justice rather than to wield clemency as a parting political favor will read this favorably. The 36-0 and 36-1 Senate votes signal cross-party agreement that the governor's pardon power needs a guardrail during the politically charged period around a gubernatorial election. The narrower House vote (82-12) reflects some Democratic resistance, likely on separation-of-powers grounds. The amendment does NOT eliminate the pardon power; it imposes a roughly three-month window where it cannot be exercised. Treason cases and reprieves remain unaffected.
