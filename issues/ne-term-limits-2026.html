Nebraska Legislative Term Limits Amendment (LR19CA, Nov 2026)

Statewide — All Nebraska Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Extends the limit on consecutive service in Nebraska's Legislature from two four-year terms (eight years) to three four-year terms (twelve years).
  • A senator who serves the maximum would still face the existing sit-out before becoming eligible again.
  • It loosens, rather than removes, the term limit voters wrote into the constitution in 2000.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Legislatively referred constitutional amendment carried in Legislative Resolution 19CA; primary sponsor State Sen. Rob Dover (District 19). Nebraska's Legislature is unicameral and officially nonpartisan.
  • Reported vote: the Legislature adopted the referral 39-10 on May 28, 2025 (a referral requires a 60 percent supermajority, or 30 of 49 votes).
  • Placed on the November 3, 2026 ballot; voters last set the eight-year limit in 2000.

Who Supports This

  • State Sen. Rob Dover (District 19), sponsor
  • The legislative majority that referred it (39-10)

Who Opposes This

  • The ten senators who voted against the referral

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

Term limits were the people's own check on entrenched power, written into the constitution by direct vote in 2000 precisely so that no lawmaker could settle in for a career. This amendment does not abolish that check, but it does relax it by half a term. The honest case for the change is that eight years is barely enough time to learn a complex job and that experience is handed instead to lobbyists and permanent staff. The honest case against is that the longer a man holds a seat, the safer the incumbency and the harder the unseating, and that the voters already weighed this and chose eight years. Either way, the deciding question belongs to the citizen who set the limit in the first place: does Nebraska gain more in seasoned legislators than it risks in entrenched ones. Weigh it on accountability, not on any one senator.
