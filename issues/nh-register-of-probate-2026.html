New Hampshire Abolish Office of Register of Probate Amendment (Question 1, Nov 2026)

Statewide — All New Hampshire Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Removes every reference to the office of register of probate from the New Hampshire Constitution.
  • The office's substantive duties were already transferred in the 2011 court reorganization, leaving the constitutional references obsolete.
  • It is administrative housekeeping, not a change to any policy, court power, or citizen right.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Legislatively referred constitutional amendment carried in CACR 13 (2026 session); sponsor Rep. Dennis Mannion (R). New Hampshire has no citizen-initiative process — amendments require a three-fifths vote of each chamber to refer, then two-thirds of voters to ratify.
  • Reported votes: House 325-15 (Feb 5, 2026); Senate 23-1 (Mar 26, 2026) — both well above the three-fifths referral threshold.
  • A near-identical measure in 2022 won 62.9 percent voter support but fell short of the two-thirds needed to ratify.

Who Supports This

  • Rep. Dennis Mannion (R), sponsor
  • The bipartisan legislative supermajorities that referred it (House 325-15, Senate 23-1)

Who Opposes This

  • The handful of legislators who voted no (15 representatives and 1 senator)

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

This is about as low-stakes as a constitutional amendment gets. The office it deletes has been a dead letter since 2011, and the change touches no power, tax, or liberty — it merely cleans obsolete language out of the state charter. There is a quiet virtue in keeping the founding document honest and uncluttered, so that what it says matches what actually governs. The only real friction is the two-thirds bar: a sensible cleanup like this failed in 2022 not because voters opposed it but because too few bothered to vote yes. For the citizen, the question is simply whether to tidy a vestige the legislature has already retired by an overwhelming, bipartisan margin.
