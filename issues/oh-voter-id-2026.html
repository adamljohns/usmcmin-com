Ohio Voter Photo ID Constitutional Amendment (Nov 2026)

Statewide — All Ohio Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Enshrines Ohio's existing photo-ID voting requirement in the state constitution.
  • Requires in-person voters to present government-issued photo ID.
  • For absentee voters, permits either a photo ID or a signature plus another authorized unique identifier; allows the legislature to create an alternative verification process for voters with sincere religious objections to being photographed.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Legislatively referred constitutional amendment carried in Senate Joint Resolution 10 (SJR 10); sponsors Sens. Jane Timken and Theresa Gavarone.
  • Reported votes: Senate 22-9 (Jun 3, 2026); House 61-27 (Jun 10, 2026) — both clearing the 60 percent referral threshold. Certified on House passage.

Who Supports This

  • Sens. Jane Timken and Theresa Gavarone (R), sponsors
  • The legislative majorities that referred it (Senate 22-9, House 61-27)
  • Election-integrity advocates

Who Opposes This

  • The nine senators and 27 representatives who voted no
  • Voting-access advocates who oppose constitutionalizing photo-ID rules

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

Ohio already requires photo ID by statute; this would lock it into the constitution so a future legislature or court couldn't easily undo it. For citizens who see voter ID as a basic, common-sense safeguard of election integrity, putting it beyond the reach of ordinary politics is the appeal. Opponents argue ID rules can burden eligible voters and that constitutionalizing them is rigid; the religious-objection carve-out is meant to soften that edge. The votes split largely along party lines. If election integrity ranks high for you and you trust the existing ID framework, this points yes; the live questions are constitutional rigidity and access, not the ID requirement itself, which is already law.
