Oklahoma State Question 844 — Manufacturing Tax Exemption Reimbursement Methodology Amendment (Aug 2026)

Statewide — All Oklahoma Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Directs the Legislature to establish a set methodology for calculating how much the state owes local governments and school districts to cover revenue lost to the manufacturing exemption.
  • The exemption itself — a statewide five-year ad valorem (property) tax break for qualifying manufacturing facilities — is not changed; only the way the state backfills local budgets is fixed in the constitution.
  • Aims to make the reimbursement predictable rather than left to year-to-year fund balances.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Legislatively referred constitutional amendment carried in House Joint Resolution 1087 (2026 session). Oklahoma constitutional amendments reach the ballot by legislative referral or by initiative petition; this one is a referral.
  • Reported votes: House 76-16 (Mar 25, 2026); Senate 38-7 (Apr 9, 2026).
  • Placed on the August 25, 2026 primary-runoff ballot as State Question 844.

Who Supports This

  • Rep. Kyle Hilbert (R-29), House sponsor
  • The bipartisan legislative majorities that referred it (House 76-16, Senate 38-7)

Who Opposes This

  • No organized opposition campaign recorded in available sources; 16 House and 7 Senate members voted no

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

This is plumbing, not policy — but the kind of plumbing that decides whether a school district can plan its budget. When the state hands manufacturers a five-year tax holiday, the local school and county still have bills to pay, and the state is supposed to make them whole. Right now that reimbursement rides on a fund balance instead of a fixed formula. Writing a methodology into the constitution is a modest, sensible move toward keeping the state's promise to local communities. A citizen weighing it should ask whether the promised formula will actually be funded — the amendment requires a method, not a dollar amount.
