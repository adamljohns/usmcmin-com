November 3, 2026 On the 2026 Ballot

Tennessee SJR 25 — Remove Right to Bail for Certain Serious Offenses Amendment (Nov 2026)

Statewide — All Tennessee Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Expands the offenses for which a judge may deny bail ('when the proof is evident or the presumption great') beyond capital crimes.
  • Covers terrorism, second-degree murder, aggravated rape of a child, aggravated rape, grave torture, and any offense for which a convicted defendant could not be released before serving at least 85 percent of the sentence.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Legislatively referred constitutional amendment (Senate Joint Resolution 25) that cleared two consecutive General Assemblies.
  • Verified second-passage votes: Senate 23-6 (Mar 17, 2025); House 84-10 (Apr 21, 2025). First-passage (2023-24) counts were not independently confirmed for this entry.
  • To pass, 'yes' votes must exceed 50 percent of the total votes cast for governor on November 3, 2026.

Who Supports This

  • Bipartisan legislative majorities (Senate 23-6, House 84-10)

Who Opposes This

  • Rep. Justin Pearson (D), who warned of disproportionate impact on poor, Black, and Latino defendants

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

Today a Tennessee judge can deny bail only for capital crimes; this widens that to a named list of grave offenses. The case for it is plain — keep the genuinely dangerous off the street before trial. The caution, raised on the floor by Rep. Justin Pearson, is that pre-trial detention can fall hardest on poor and minority defendants who haven't been convicted of anything. Both are true. The amendment limits the new discretion to enumerated serious felonies rather than leaving it open-ended, which is the right way to do this if it's done at all.
