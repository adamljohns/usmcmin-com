Vermont Proposal 3 — Right to Collective Bargaining Amendment (Nov 2026)

Statewide — All Vermont Voters

What The Amendment Does

  • Establishes a state constitutional right for employees to organize and join a labor organization for collective bargaining over wages, hours, and working conditions.
  • Prohibits laws that 'interfere with, negate, or diminish' that right — which would effectively bar Vermont from enacting right-to-work laws.
  • Critics note it could, in some cases, constrain an individual employee's ability to bargain separately from the union.

How It Got On The Ballot

  • Constitutional amendment (Proposal 3 / PR0003) that passed two consecutive biennial sessions of the General Assembly.
  • First passage (2023-24): Senate 29-0; House 129-8 (April 2024).
  • Final passage (2025-26): Senate 29-0 (Mar 20, 2025); House 125-15 (May 1, 2025), placing it on the ballot.

Who Supports This

  • Vermont AFL-CIO, VSEA, Vermont Democratic Party
  • House Speaker Jill Krowinski (D); Senate President Pro Tem Philip Baruth (D)

Who Opposes This

  • Rob Roper (former Vermont Republican Party chair), who argues it can strip an individual's right to bargain on their own behalf

RESOLUTE Citizen Analysis

This writes a union right into Vermont's constitution and, in doing so, permanently forecloses any future right-to-work law there. For organized labor it's a landmark. From a conservative economic lens there are two cautions: it locks one side of a contested economic debate into the constitution, where voters can't easily revisit it, and critics like Rob Roper argue it can override an individual worker's right to bargain on his own terms. Vermont is not a right-to-work state today, so the real change here is permanence, not present policy.
