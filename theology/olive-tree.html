Theological Dictionary

Olive Tree

The single, continuous covenant people of God across all of history.

Reformed Covenant Theology — Romans 11

Jew & Gentile Olive Tree

Definition

/ˈɒl.ɪv triː/

Olive Tree from Latin oliva (olive) and Old English treow (tree). The Hebrew zayit (olive) is one of the seven species of the Promised Land.

Deeply embedded in biblical symbolism, its oil fueled the temple lampstand and anointed kings and priests. The Apostle Paul uses the cultivated olive tree as the definitive metaphor for God's singular covenant people across all of history.

📘 Biblical Definition

The olive tree in Romans 11 represents the continuous, unified covenant people of God. The root is the Abrahamic Covenant—God's promises of grace. The branches represent those in the visible covenant community.

Paul explains that natural branches (ethnic Israelites) were broken off strictly due to unbelief, not ethnicity. Simultaneously, wild branches (believing Gentiles) are grafted in by faith. The tree demonstrates that God does not have two separate peoples; He has one tree. Salvation is found only by being attached to this single root of grace through faith in Jesus Christ.

📝 Webster 1828 Definition

OLIVE: A plant or tree of the genus Olea, cultivated for its fruit and the oil it produces.

📘 Key Scripture

And if some of the branches were broken off, while you, a wild olive shoot, were grafted in among the others and now share in the nourishing root of the olive tree, do not be arrogant toward the branches. If you are arrogant, consider this: you do not support the root, but the root supports you.

— Romans 11:17–18 (ESV)

They were broken off because of their unbelief, but you stand fast through faith. So do not become proud, but stand in awe.

— Romans 11:20 (ESV)

But I am like a green olive tree in the house of God. I trust in the steadfast love of God forever and ever.

— Psalm 52:8 (ESV)

The Lord once called you 'a green olive tree, beautiful with good fruit.' But with the roar of a mighty storm he will set it on fire, and its branches will be broken.

— Jeremiah 11:16 (ESV)

⚠ Modern Corruption

The metaphor of the Olive Tree is frequently ignored by systems like Dispensationalism, which illogically require two trees—one for Israel and one for the Church. The text of Romans 11 refutes this by showing Gentiles are grafted into the same historical tree that the patriarchs belonged to.

Furthermore, the tree refutes Universalism and Ethnic Entitlement, proving that natural descent guarantees nothing; branches are sustained solely by active faith in Christ. There is no distinction between Jew and Gentile in access to the root—both come by the same means: faith alone.

Usage

  • "The olive tree of Romans 11 proves there is only one continuous covenant people of God throughout redemptive history."
  • "Gentile believers are not planted in a new, separate garden; they are grafted directly into the rich root of the olive tree alongside the faithful Jewish remnant."
